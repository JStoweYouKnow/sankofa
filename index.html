The challenge

The 1870 Brick Wall

The 1870 federal census is the first to list formerly enslaved Black Americans by their own full names. Before it, enslaved people appeared in official records only as property — the 1850 and 1860 Slave Schedules recorded the enslaver's name, ages, and a count, but never the individuals themselves. For millions of families, this is where the paper trail runs out.

"Getting past 1870 usually requires working sideways through indirect records — Freedmen's Bureau files, Freedman's Bank registers, cohabitation bonds, and the enslaver's own papers — rather than a single record that names your ancestor directly."

The records aren't gone. Freedmen's Bureau field offices kept labor contracts, marriage registers, and letters from people searching for family sold away from them. Freedman's Bank branches kept depositor registers with birthplace, former enslaver, spouse, and siblings. Cohabitation bonds recorded formerly enslaved couples' own words about their families. Forebear surfaces all of it.

What Forebear does

Every tool the research demands

Built specifically for the records and methods that matter in Black American genealogy — not a general-purpose tree builder.

📋

Guided Research Plan

Pick an ancestor and work backwards — step by step. Anchor them in the 1870 census, locate their county's Freedmen's Bureau field office, check the Bank and cohabitation records, identify enslaver candidates. Every step includes prefilled searches and one-click log entries so nothing slips through.

Per-person · Persistent progress
🔍

Discovery — 80+ Archives

Enter a name, place, and time period — Discovery builds prefilled deep links into the Freedmen's Bureau, Freedman's Bank, USCT pensions, state archives across the South, Last Seen reunion ads, Caribbean records, Canadian Black Loyalist registers, and the Atlantic slave trade databases. Name variants are searched too.

One search · Dozens of collections
🌍

Bridge to Africa

Push the line as far as documents allow, then map what DNA, ethnonyms, and Atlantic voyage records can add. The Bridge to Africa workspace holds your DNA results, ethnonym glossary lookups, Slave Voyages embarkation data, and African Origins research — all tagged by confidence level so you know what's proven and what's a working hypothesis.

DNA + Documents + Oral tradition
📖

Research Log + Field Guide

Track every source you consult — hit or miss. A dead end recorded today saves you from re-searching it in three years. The built-in Field Guide covers the 1870 brick wall, Freedmen's Bureau and Bank records, cohabitation bonds, USCT pensions, WPA narratives, and the step-by-step path toward Africa — in plain language, not genealogist jargon.
